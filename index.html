The Philippine Flood Crisis: Why It Persists

The Philippines' annual flood crisis is not merely a consequence of typhoons or heavy monsoon rains—it is a direct result of systemic corruption, institutional neglect, and a profound lack of accountability within its infrastructure governance. For decades, billions have been allocated for flood mitigation, yet communities continue to drown—not because of nature's wrath, but because of man-made failure.

In July 2025, President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. stunned the nation by revealing that only 15 contractors out of 2,000 had cornered nearly 20% of over ₱500 billion in flood control spending since 2022. What followed was not just an audit—but a national reckoning. Senate hearings exposed "ghost projects" worth hundreds of millions, contractors who never built anything, and officials who signed off on non-existent structures.

As Senator Risa Hontiveros put it during a Blue Ribbon Committee hearing: "May mga proyekto tuloy na imbes makabawas sa baha, nagdulot pa ng mas malaking baha… May ghost projects din daw na hindi makita, hindi mahanap ng LGU kahit ngayon." ("There are projects that instead of reducing floods, caused bigger ones… There are ghost projects that even LGUs cannot find today.")

DPWH Secretary Vince Dizon, when asked if there was a master plan, simply replied: "Wala po." No unified strategy. No watershed-level coordination. No oversight. Just contracts—awarded, paid, and vanished.

The human cost is staggering. A ₱100 million project in Lucena, Quezon, collapsed during a storm, killing families. In Bulacan, President Marcos held up a report for a ₱55.7 million riverwall project—only to reveal it didn't exist. "A lousy flood control project that collapsed during the flood that killed a family — I mean, how can you live with that?" he said in his podcast. "I can't live with it."

The economic toll is equally devastating. Finance Secretary Ralph Recto estimated that anomalous flood projects cost the economy ₱118 billion between 2023 and 2025. The stock market plunged. Foreign investors paused. South Korea suspended a $503 million loan. And still, the money flowed—to shell companies, to kickbacks, to luxury cars owned by contractors whose projects were never completed.

Yet, hope remains. Thousands rallied at EDSA Shrine. Students walked out of universities. Business groups demanded independent investigations. The Independent Commission for Infrastructure (ICI) was formed. Over 130 bank accounts were frozen. And for the first time in years, the public is watching—and demanding justice.

This is not about weather. This is about willpower. About integrity. About choosing people over profit. The flood crisis persists because we allowed it to. But it can end—if we choose to hold those responsible accountable.

Core Issues and Systemic Flaws

Corruption & Kickbacks

"Ghost projects," substandard construction, and inflated contracts drain public funds. Audits suggest up to 40% of budgets vanish as kickbacks—leaving real infrastructure dangerously underfunded.

Fragmented Planning

Projects lack watershed-wide coordination. Solutions often shift floodwaters downstream instead of systemic risk reduction—wasting billions and endangering more communities.

Neglect of Nature-Based Solutions

Over-reliance on concrete dikes ignores cost-effective, resilient green infrastructure—like wetland restoration and urban greening—that deliver ecological and social co-benefits.
